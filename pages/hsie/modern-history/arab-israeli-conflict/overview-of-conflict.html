Overview of the Arab-Israeli Conflict

The Arab-Israeli conflict is a complex and ongoing struggle between the Jewish state of Israel and the Arab populations in the surrounding regions, particularly the Palestinians. The conflict has deep historical roots and has led to multiple wars and ongoing tensions.

The historical background of the Arab-Israeli conflict is rooted in the late Ottoman period, British colonial policies, and the rise of nationalism among both Jews and Arabs in the early 20th century.

  • Ottoman Rule: The region was under Ottoman control until the end of World War I, with a diverse population of Muslims, Christians, and Jews.
  • British Mandate: After WWI, the British took control of Palestine, leading to increased tensions between Jews and Arabs as Jewish immigration grew.
  • UN Partition Plan: In 1947, the UN proposed a partition plan to create separate Jewish and Arab states, leading to the 1948 Arab-Israeli War.

The conflict has been marked by several key events that have shaped its course, including wars, peace efforts, and changes in leadership.

  • 1948 Arab-Israeli War: Following the British withdrawal and the declaration of the state of Israel, a war broke out, leading to significant territorial changes and the displacement of Palestinians.
  • Six-Day War (1967): Israel captured significant territories, including the West Bank, Gaza Strip, and East Jerusalem, increasing tensions with Arab states.
  • Intifadas: Palestinian uprisings against Israeli occupation, particularly in the late 1980s and early 2000s, highlighted the ongoing struggle for Palestinian statehood.
